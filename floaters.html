Understanding Floaters

An in‑depth look at vitreous floaters—their causes, symptoms, and treatment options

What are Floaters?

Get a comprehensive understanding of the nature and causes of vitreous floaters.

Floaters are small, moving spots that appear in your field of vision. They can look like dark specks, strings, or cobwebs. Most floaters are caused by age-related changes in the vitreous, a gel-like substance that fills the inside of your eye. As you get older, the vitreous becomes more liquid, and microscopic fibers within it can clump together, casting shadows on your retina.

Common Symptoms

  • Seeing small specks or spots that drift across your vision
  • Noticing the spots move when you move your eyes
  • Seeing the floaters more clearly when looking at a bright background, such as a blue sky or a white wall

Causes of Floaters

Explore the various factors that contribute to the development of floaters.

While most floaters are benign, it's important to understand the potential causes:

  • Aging: The most common cause, as the vitreous changes over time.
  • Posterior Vitreous Detachment (PVD): A condition where the vitreous separates from the retina.
  • Eye Trauma or Surgery: Can sometimes lead to increased floaters.
  • Inflammation: Conditions like uveitis can cause floaters.
  • Retinal Tears or Detachment: These are serious conditions that can cause a sudden increase in floaters.
  • Other Causes: Less common causes include diabetes, and deposits in the eye.

Diagnosis of Floaters

Learn about the methods used to diagnose and evaluate floaters.

A comprehensive eye exam is crucial for diagnosing floaters and ruling out any serious underlying conditions.

Diagnostic Procedures

  • Visual Acuity Test: To assess your overall vision.
  • Slit-Lamp Examination: A detailed examination of the front of your eye.
  • Dilated Fundus Examination: Eye drops are used to widen your pupil, allowing the doctor to see the back of your eye (retina and vitreous) in detail. This is the most important part of the exam for evaluating floaters.
  • Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): An imaging technique that provides detailed cross-sectional views of the retina and vitreous.

Treatment Options

Explore the available treatment options for floaters, ranging from observation to surgical intervention.

Most floaters do not require treatment. However, if they are significantly affecting your vision, your ophthalmologist may recommend one of the following:

Treatment Options

In many cases, floaters become less noticeable over time as the brain adapts to them. Your ophthalmologist will monitor your condition with regular eye exams.

A laser is used to break up large floaters, making them less noticeable. This procedure is not suitable for all types of floaters and carries some risks.

A surgical procedure to remove the vitreous gel and replace it with a saline solution. This is typically reserved for severe cases where floaters significantly impair vision and other treatments are not suitable. Vitrectomy carries significant risks, including retinal detachment, cataract, and infection.

When to Worry About Floaters

Recognizing the signs and symptoms that require immediate medical attention.

While most floaters are harmless, a sudden increase in floaters, especially when accompanied by other symptoms, can indicate a serious eye condition that requires immediate attention.

Seek Immediate Medical Attention If You Experience:

  • A sudden increase in the number of floaters
  • Flashes of light in the same eye as the floaters
  • A shadow or curtain-like blockage in your vision
  • Sudden loss of vision
  • Eye pain

These symptoms may indicate a retinal tear or detachment, which can lead to permanent vision loss if not treated promptly.
