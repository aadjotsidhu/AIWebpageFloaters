Understanding Floaters

An in‑depth look at vitreous floaters—their causes, symptoms, and treatment options

What Are Floaters?

Floaters are tiny clumps of collagen or other debris in the vitreous, casting shadows on the retina. They are often a natural part of aging, but can sometimes indicate pathology.

Diagnosis & Management

Diagnosis involves comprehensive ocular examinations, including dilated fundus exam and OCT imaging. Management may vary from watchful waiting to surgical intervention.

In-Depth: What Are Floaters?

Floaters result from changes in the vitreous humor’s collagen structure. As the vitreous ages, it liquefies and its collagen fibers clump together, casting shadows on the retina. In some cases, floaters can be symptomatic of other ocular conditions. Detailed clinical studies have outlined their prevalence, correlation with age-related vitreous changes, and potential interventions.

Diagnosis & Management Strategies

Diagnostic techniques include detailed slit-lamp examination, dilated fundus exam, and optical coherence tomography (OCT). Management strategies range from reassurance and monitoring to interventional procedures such as Nd:YAG laser vitreolysis or vitrectomy in severe cases.

Posterior Vitreous Detachment & Retinal Breaks

Understanding the cascade from PVD to retinal breaks and lattice degeneration

What is PVD?

Posterior Vitreous Detachment (PVD) is the separation of the vitreous from the retina, a common process that increases with age but can be accelerated by trauma or myopia.

Detailed research shows that early detection of PVD can significantly reduce the risk of subsequent retinal tears. Techniques such as OCT and ultrasonography are essential in evaluating the extent of vitreous detachment.

Retinal Breaks & Lattice Degeneration

Retinal breaks may develop as a consequence of vitreoretinal traction during PVD. Lattice degeneration is a related condition marked by thinning of the retina and predisposition to tears.

The guidelines recommend immediate intervention for symptomatic tears using laser photocoagulation or cryotherapy. Lattice degeneration management focuses on regular monitoring, especially in high-risk patients.
